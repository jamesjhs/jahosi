Project

splash!

Playpool thermal and cost planner · Global weather inputs · Browser-local exports

splash! is a planning tool for temporary and lightly installed outdoor playpools. It estimates warm-up effort, overnight cooling, and likely running cost by combining pool geometry, heater setup, tariffs, cover assumptions, and weather context.

The app was designed to bridge a practical gap: many pool calculators are either too generic or too simplistic. splash! focuses on playpool-specific planning, supports manual inputs at every stage, and blends cost planning with thermodynamic behaviour in one workflow.

Live planning flow

  1. 1. Set pool geometry, heater, and tariff schedule
  2. 2. Enter 3-day forecast manually or auto-fill from global location search
  3. 3. Review 36-hour and 3-day simulation outputs
  4. 4. Compare annual and season-level cost projections

Core function

splash! helps answer practical questions before you spend: can your heater reach and hold your target range, how much cover strategy changes outcomes, and what annual/season operating cost you should expect under your selected setup.

Key features

  • Manual forecast workflow that always remains editable.
  • Global place search (country/city/town) for weather auto-fill via Open-Meteo geocoding.
  • Auto-fill of 3-day daily weather plus 36-hour hourly weather for short-range simulation.
  • Location-aware monthly climate profiling with fallback baseline behaviour when archive data is unavailable.
  • 3-day stepped simulation, 36-hour pool temperature chart, and monthly maintenance/cost analysis.
  • Season planner (pool in / pool away dates) with electricity + water-fill cost summary.
  • Local exports (summary/JSON/CSV) and browser-side configuration persistence.

What makes it distinct

Compared with common pool-cost calculators, splash! is unusual in combining manual-first control, global weather + climate adaptation, and practical forecast/cost outputs tailored to temporary playpool use. It also keeps outputs transparent with model checks, explicit assumptions, and locally generated exports for planning rather than black-box estimates.
